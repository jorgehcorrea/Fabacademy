Creating a chair-joint plug or cover

I require a simple yet usable mold and casting part. I developed a very simple cover for a join in the rocking chair done in week 8 - controlled cutting

In this case, I edited the multibody part done in the previous assignment and exported it into a new file. Inside I primed the file for casting. Adding mostly 5deg draft and Raduis curves. Aditionally I added a text 'FAB' to add a bit more complexity.

My instructor @Gallo guided me into creating a single side mold and part usng and rearanging efficiently every feature in order to focure in the actual produciton of the week. Im hopping to get a slick finished cover.

Here are the design notes and modifications done.

This is the final part.

In between I also decided to test a sample in 3D print cause .. the printer was next to me.

So solidworks had draft feature: looked too good to be true. It was. It actually added about 2mm per side.. so the final part was not close to fitting in its holes. The full joint hole was 6mm and the final part was 8+ mm. I enede reding and printing the test. Good thing I Tested it out in the 3D Print.

To fix it I used the 'draft' feature option inside the extrude feature.

Once the cad was fixed, it became time for the pre-machining. In this case I used jewler's wax.

Creating the mold

Creating the mold with the wax was fun. I used a wax ment for jewllery that holds its shape well. Its sturdy and typically used for the lost wax process. In this case I will be pouring silicon and later solid plastic.

Because the material is used in the hand-crafted area by popular craftmen in Ecuador, it does not contain much information other the salesmen provide. It is melted as wax does and take a couple of hours to fully set. In this case it was melted in a Tupperware about the same size of the part Ill be machining.

In this step of the process I grew impacient and as a result had to repete the meltin process several times. By the end of the day I was an expert.
